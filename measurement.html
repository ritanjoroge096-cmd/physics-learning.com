üìò KCSE Physics Form 1: Measurement (Expanded Notes)

1. Introduction to Measurement

Measurement is the process of comparing a physical quantity with a standard unit. It allows scientists and learners to quantify observations, conduct experiments, and communicate results with precision. In physics, accurate measurement is essential for validating theories, conducting experiments, and solving real-world problems.

2. Physical Quantities

Base Quantities: Length, Mass, Time, Temperature, Electric Current, Luminous Intensity, Amount of Substance.

Derived Quantities: Area, Volume, Density, Speed, Acceleration, Force.

3. SI Units and Prefixes

  • Metre (m) ‚Äì Length
  • Kilogram (kg) ‚Äì Mass
  • Second (s) ‚Äì Time
  • Kelvin (K) ‚Äì Temperature
  • Ampere (A) ‚Äì Electric Current
  • Prefixes: milli (10‚Åª¬≥), centi (10‚Åª¬≤), kilo (10¬≥)

4. Instruments and Their Uses

  • Ruler ‚Äì Measures straight lengths (least count: 1 mm)
  • Vernier Calipers ‚Äì Measures internal/external diameters (least count: 0.1 mm)
  • Micrometer Screw Gauge ‚Äì Measures small thicknesses (least count: 0.01 mm)
  • Beam Balance ‚Äì Measures mass
  • Stopwatch ‚Äì Measures time intervals
  • Measuring Cylinder ‚Äì Measures volume of liquids

5. Errors in Measurement

  • Parallax Error: Eye not level with scale
  • Zero Error: Instrument doesn‚Äôt read zero when it should
  • Systematic Error: Faulty calibration
  • Random Error: Human reaction or environmental factors

6. Significant Figures

Significant figures reflect the precision of a measurement. Rules:

  • All non-zero digits are significant
  • Zeros between significant digits are significant
  • Leading zeros are not significant
  • Trailing zeros in decimals are significant

7. Measuring Volume of Irregular Objects

Use a Eureka can:

  1. Fill with water until it overflows
  2. Let overflow stop
  3. Drop object gently
  4. Collect displaced water in measuring cylinder
  5. Volume of displaced water = volume of object

8. KCSE Exam Tips

  • Always state units clearly
  • Use correct number of significant figures
  • Show working in calculations
  • Label diagrams accurately
  • Practice past paper questions regularly

1. Name two instruments used to measure length and state their least count.

Ruler (least count: 1 mm), Vernier calipers (least count: 0.1 mm)

2. State two sources of error when measuring length using a ruler.

Parallax error, worn-out edges, misalignment

3. A student measures a wire with a micrometer screw gauge and gets 1.25 mm. The actual diameter is 1.30 mm. Calculate the percentage error.

Error = 0.05 mm; % error = (0.05 / 1.30) √ó 100 = 3.85%

4. Describe how to measure the volume of an irregular object using a Eureka can.

Fill Eureka can, let overflow stop, drop object, collect displaced water in measuring cylinder = volume

5. Define density and state its SI unit.

Density = mass/volume; SI unit = kg/m¬≥

üìö Physics Glossary

  • Accuracy: Closeness to the true value
  • Precision: Repeatability of measurements
  • SI Unit: Standard unit of measurement
  • Parallax: Apparent shift due to eye position
  • Zero Error: Instrument not reading zero
  • Density: Mass per unit volume
  • Volume: Space occupied by an object
  • Mass: Quantity of matter in an object
  • Length: Distance between two points
  • Time: Duration between two events
  • Temperature: Measure of thermal energy
  • Systematic Error: Consistent measurement error
  • Random Error: Unpredictable variation
  • Instrument: Tool used for measurement
  • Calibration: Adjusting instrument accuracy
  • Reading: Value shown by an instrument
  • Derived Quantity: Quantity from two or more base units
  • Area: Surface covered by a shape
  • Micrometer: Measures small thicknesses
  • Vernier Calipers: Measures internal/external diameters
